In 1999, the Project for Excellence in Journalism — working with Jay Rosen at NYU and Princeton Survey Research Associates — produced what remains the most cited content analysis in American journalism studies. "[Framing the News](https://www.pewresearch.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/8/legacy/framingthenews.pdf)" coded 2,269 front-page stories from seven US newspapers, asking a deceptively simple set of questions: What narrative frames do journalists use? What triggers a story into existence? What subliminal messages does the news carry?

That study has never been replicated for India. Until now.

We adapted PEJ's methodology for Indian digital news in 2026, coding 170 homepage stories from 12 English-language outlets using a constructed week spanning January through May. The outlets: Scroll.in, The Wire, The Print, Newslaundry, FirstPost, NDTV, India Today, Hindustan Times, Indian Express, Times of India, News18, and The Hindu. The coding taxonomy: 14 narrative frames, 16 topic categories, 12 trigger types, and 9 underlying message categories — all adapted from PEJ's original codebook with one India-specific addition.

The findings rearrange several assumptions about how Indian digital journalism works.

## The inverted pyramid is not dead

Straight news — the who-what-when-where-why inverted pyramid with no dominant narrative frame — accounts for 27.6% of all homepage stories. In PEJ's American study, that figure was just 16%. Indian digital journalism, taken as a whole, is more fact-based than American newspapers were at the turn of the century.

But this aggregate masks a structural fracture.

## Two Indias

Split the 12 outlets into digital natives (Scroll, Wire, Print, Newslaundry, FirstPost) and legacy-digital operations (NDTV, India Today, HT, Indian Express, TOI, News18, The Hindu), and the numbers diverge violently. Legacy outlets use straight news framing 37.1% of the time. Digital natives use it 12.3% of the time — a 3:1 ratio.

This is not a quality gap. It is a philosophical divide. Legacy outlets default to reporting what happened. Digital natives default to interrogating what it means. The Wire frames 27% of its homepage stories around conflict. NDTV frames 67% as straight news. These are not the same species of journalism wearing different clothes.

## Combative framing: close to PEJ, differently distributed

PEJ's most famous finding was that three "combative" frames — conflict, horse race, and wrongdoing exposed — accounted for 30% of all American front-page stories, twice the number of straight news accounts. In India, the equivalent figure is 25.2% (conflict 10.0%, horse race 7.6%, wrongdoing 7.6%). Close — but distributed differently. In the US, combativeness clustered around government reporting. In India, it concentrates in election coverage. When elections are the trigger, horse-race framing shoots to 58.3% — the frame becomes almost reflexive.

## The consensus desert

Here is the devastating number: 1.2%.

That is the share of Indian digital news stories framed around points of agreement. Two stories out of 170. In PEJ's American study — which itself noted the poverty of consensus framing — the figure was 6%. India's number is one-tenth of that.

What this means: Indian digital journalism has almost completely abandoned the narrative device of building stories around where stakeholders agree. In a polity that has just conducted five simultaneous state elections, is navigating a US tariff war, managing fallout from a Middle East conflict affecting its oil supply, and processing post-election violence in Bengal — the press cannot find a single story worth telling through the lens of common ground.

This is not a finding about Indian society. It is a finding about Indian journalism's storytelling reflexes.

## The enterprise finding

PEJ found that when American journalists initiated stories through their own enterprise — rather than responding to government statements or breaking events — they defaulted to personality profiles and trend stories. In India, the pattern is different. When Indian digital journalists go out and get the story themselves, the dominant frame is wrongdoing exposed (25%). Indian enterprise journalism is accountability journalism. It does not profile the powerful; it investigates them.

This is, arguably, the healthiest single finding in the study.

## Transparency predicts accountability

We extended the PEJ framework with two additional layers drawn from Jay Rosen's work on journalistic authority and Mark Deuze's structural typology of online journalism. Each outlet was scored on a "Rosen Transparency Index" — measuring disclosed funding models, corrections policies, editorial stance transparency, and audience relationship.

The correlation is near-perfect. Newslaundry (Rosen score 10/10, fully reader-funded, complete transparency) devotes 80% of its editorial output to accountability frames. Scroll.in (7/10) runs 40%. NDTV, Hindustan Times, Times of India, and FirstPost — all corporate-funded, all employing what Rosen calls "the view from nowhere" — produce zero accountability framing on their homepages.

The funding model is the frame. The audience relationship is the editorial posture. This is not a matter of individual editorial courage; it is structural.

## The Hindu: policy journalism's outlier

One outlet breaks the legacy mould entirely. The Hindu devotes 26.7% of its homepage to policy-explored framing — five times the field average of 5.3%. It is the only legacy outlet to produce a consensus-framed story in the entire sample. Its historical-outlook framing (13.3%) is nearly triple the field average. In a landscape where legacy digital defaults to straight news and digital natives default to conflict or accountability, The Hindu defaults to explanation. It asks what a policy means rather than who wins or who is to blame. Whether this reflects the paper's 148-year editorial tradition, its Chennai base, or its particular ownership structure is beyond this study's scope — but the fingerprint is distinctive and immediately visible in the radar chart.

## What the government drives

Government statements and actions trigger 27.1% of all stories — the single largest trigger category, identical to PEJ's finding a quarter-century ago. The Indian press, like the American press of 1999, remains fundamentally reactive to the state. But newsroom enterprise is second at 14.1%, and when enterprise is the trigger, the resulting frame is overwhelmingly accountability-oriented. The capacity exists. Whether it is deployed depends on who pays the bills.

## The underlying messages

PEJ found that when American stories carried identifiable underlying messages, optimism was the most common theme (27%). India's figure is 29.4%. Anti-establishment messages — distrust of systems, belief that institutions are failing — account for 12.9%, but cluster almost entirely in digital-native outlets (24.6% in digital natives vs 4.4% in legacy). Legacy Indian news carries optimism. Digital-native Indian news carries suspicion. The reader chooses their epistemic frame when they choose their outlet.

## What this means for the Indian press

Three conclusions for editors and media researchers:

The straight news default in legacy outlets is not a sign of health — it may indicate an inability or unwillingness to provide interpretive, analytical, and accountability journalism. When NDTV runs 67% straight news on its homepage, it is not being rigorous. It is being safe.

The extinction of consensus framing is an editorial failure that the profession has not discussed. If every story is told through conflict, horse race, or wrongdoing — and never through agreement, collaboration, or policy convergence — the press is structurally incapable of narrating the resolution of problems. Only their existence.

And the transparency-accountability correlation should terrify any corporate-funded newsroom claiming editorial independence. The data says otherwise. The data says your frame is your funding.
